Cupar Grain Silo

Cupar, Fife · Scotland
Reverberation Time
37.5 seconds
The Recording

The Cupar Grain Silo is a decommissioned 200ft concrete tower on the outskirts of Cupar, Fife. Its cylindrical form creates an extraordinary acoustic environment — sound reflecting off the curved walls and travelling the full height of the structure, producing a reverberation time of 37.5 seconds.

An impulse response was captured inside the silo and used as the foundation for a piece composed entirely around its acoustic characteristics. The 'dry' recording was then brought back into the silo and performed in front of a live audience — making the building itself the final instrument.

The recording was subsequently released as a vinyl record by Blackford Hill Records and received airplay on BBC Radio 3 Night Tracks, 6Music and BBC Radio Scotland.

Acoustic History

The Cupar Cooperative Society's grain store was built in the early 20th century to service the agricultural industry of north-east Fife. The cylindrical reinforced concrete construction — typical of industrial grain storage of the era — was designed purely for function: to keep grain dry and separated from vermin.

The geometry that made it ideal for grain storage — tall, narrow, sealed — is exactly what creates its acoustic extremity. Unlike a cathedral, where the reverberation is diffuse and musical by design, the silo's reverb is raw, industrial, almost geological. Sound decays slowly not because the building was built for it, but despite everything it was built for.

The building has been decommissioned for decades. It stands as a monument to a now largely mechanised agricultural industry, its interior silence broken only by the occasional visitor.

"We all love reverberation. As kids, we play in it — yelling in forests and caves, surreptitiously dropping objects in huge churches — mouths wide open at the lingering smears of sound which come back to us."

The Resono project exploits this sense of playing within sound by creating work designed specifically for these reverberant spaces. The effect of swimming in sound, whether in a cathedral or a disused sugar silo, promises to amaze audiences, recalling childhood experimentation and allowing new understandings of how sound behaves in space.

Prof. Peter Stollery, FRSA

Silo History

The Cupar silo, the only structure of its kind in Scotland, was built in 1964 for bulk storage of sugar, adjoining the large sugar beet processing plant in operation since 1926. Less than eight years after erection, in 1971, the factory closed, leaving many unemployed and a great deal of ill feeling.

It was subsequently used for grain storage until the early 2000s, when withdrawal of government subsidies made it no longer viable. In 2008, with the discovery of an accessible hatch into the body of the silo, its potential as a creative space was revealed. Since then, it has hosted installation, film, sound, dance and performance.

The charity Silo Projects was formed in August 2016 to encourage experimental art and appreciation of the unique industrial heritage of the Cupar Silo.

Kathy Watts — Silo Projects
